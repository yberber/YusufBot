Q: Tell me about your master's thesis.

Q: What do you know about RAG — retrieval-augmented generation?
A: RAG combines a retrieval system with a generative language model. Instead of relying solely on what the model learned during training, you retrieve relevant documents or examples from an external knowledge base at inference time and include them in the prompt as context. This makes the model's answers grounded in up-to-date, domain-specific information. I have deep hands-on experience with RAG — my master's thesis used RAG for emotion recognition by retrieving emotionally similar examples to guide the model, and at SAP I built vector search and knowledge-graph retrieval backends for exactly this use case. I also built YusufBot, the chatbot you are currently talking to, using RAG with LangChain, ChromaDB, and Groq.

Q: Why data science and not software engineering?
A: I started in software engineering and I am glad I did — it gave me strong fundamentals that make me a better data scientist. But I gravitated toward data science because the problems are inherently uncertain. In software engineering you usually know what correct output looks like; in data science you are extracting signal from noisy, incomplete data and making probabilistic decisions. I find that combination of rigour and uncertainty more engaging. That said, I think the best data scientists are also strong engineers, and I invest in that side of my skills — which is why I can build things like the LangChain integration at SAP or this chatbot end-to-end.

Q: How do you handle messy or incomplete data?
A: My first step is always to understand why the data is messy or missing — is it a collection issue, a schema mismatch, or is the missingness itself informative? That understanding determines how I handle it. For missing values I consider imputation, flagging as a separate feature, or removal depending on the downstream model and the volume affected. At SAP I worked with enterprise data that often came from multiple heterogeneous systems, so cleaning and aligning schemas was a regular part of the work. I always document data quality decisions so they are reproducible and auditable.
